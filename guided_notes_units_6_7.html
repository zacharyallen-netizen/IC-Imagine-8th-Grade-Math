What is this section about? Real-world problems often follow a linear pattern — a steady rate of change plus a starting value. We can write equations in y = mx + b form to model these situations and use them to make predictions.

Slope & Y-Intercept in Context

In real-world problems, the slope and y-intercept have specific meanings:

m (slope) = the of change — how much y changes per unit of x.

b (y-intercept) = the value — what y equals when x = .

💡 Quick check: The y-intercept is always the "before-anything-happens" value — the initial cost, starting amount, or fixed fee.

Keywords for Slope and Y-Intercept

Slope keywords (rate):

  • per hour, per mile, per day
  • each, every
  • increases by, decreases by
  • price/cost per item

Y-intercept keywords (start):

  • flat fee, base cost
  • starting amount, initial
  • up-front, deposit
  • "to begin with"

Plumber example: A plumber charges $50 to come to your house, plus $30 per hour of work.

The flat fee ($50) is paid before any work happens → b = 50
The $30 per hour is the rate of change → m = 30
Equation: y = 30x + 50

Interpret: If x = 4 hours, then y = 30(4) + 50 = $170 total.

Qualitative Graphs

What's a qualitative graph? A graph that shows the shape of a relationship over time without exact numbers. The story of the graph matters more than specific values. Common examples: distance-time graphs, temperature-time graphs.

Reading the Shape

Each section of a qualitative graph tells you something. Going left-to-right, ask:

Going UP means the quantity is .

Going DOWN means the quantity is .

FLAT (horizontal) means the quantity is (not changing).

STEEPER lines mean the change is .

SHALLOWER (gentler) lines mean the change is .

Maria rides her bike to school:

The graph shows Maria's distance from home over time. We can read the story:

  • 0 to 4 min: line goes UP at a steady rate → Maria is biking away from home toward school.
  • 4 to 6 min: line is FLAT → Maria stays at school (distance doesn't change).
  • 6 to 9 min: line goes DOWN steeper than it went up → Maria rides home faster than she went.
⚠️ Common mistake: When someone is returning home, the distance-from-home line goes DOWN (toward zero), not back up! The y-axis is "distance from start" — if you're getting closer to home, your distance is decreasing.

📐 Steps for sketching from a story:
  1. Break the story into 2–4 phases.
  2. For each phase, decide: increase, decrease, or stay the same?
  3. Decide if the change is fast (steep) or slow (shallow).
  4. Connect phases at their endpoints — no jumps in the line!

What is bivariate data? Data that has two values for each item — like a person's height AND weight, or hours studied AND test score. We use a scatterplot to look for patterns between the two variables.

Univariate vs. Bivariate

Univariate data has variable per data point. Example: a list of test scores.

Bivariate data has variables per data point. Example: hours studied AND test score for each student.

📊 The two variables together form an ordered pair (x, y) that we plot as a single dot. Each dot is one person, one observation.

What is correlation? Correlation describes how two variables tend to change together. There are three main types: positive, negative, and none. Plus two strengths: strong or weak.

Three Types of Correlation

Positive correlation: as x increases, y also . The dots trend from left to right.

Negative correlation: as x increases, y . The dots trend from left to right.

No correlation: the dots are scattered with clear pattern. As x increases, y does whatever it wants.

Strength of Correlation

Even within "positive" or "negative", the correlation can be strong (dots close to a line) or weak (dots more scattered).

Line of Best Fit

NC.8.SP.2 / NC.8.SP.3 — Informally fit a straight line for scatter plots that suggest a linear association; use the equation to solve problems and make predictions.

0 of 0 filled
What is a line of best fit? When the dots in a scatterplot suggest a linear pattern, we can draw a single straight line that goes through the "middle" of the cloud of dots. This line represents the overall trend and helps us make predictions.

When Should You Draw One?

A line of best fit only makes sense when:

The data shows a pattern (positive or negative correlation).

You should NOT draw one if the dots show correlation or a curved/non-linear pattern.

Example: The scatterplot below shows hours practiced vs. free throws made for a basketball team. The dashed green line is the line of best fit.

Notice the line:

  • Passes through the "middle" of the dots
  • Has roughly the same number of dots above and below it
  • Follows the overall positive trend
  • Doesn't have to touch any specific dot

Drawing a Line of Best Fit (Informally)

  1. Look at the overall direction of the dots (positive or negative).
  2. Use a ruler — try to put the line so dots are roughly balanced above and below.
  3. Don't connect the dots! It's a smooth straight line, not a zigzag.
  4. Don't be tricked by outliers — ignore dots far from the rest.

Using the Line to Make Predictions

Once you draw a line of best fit, you can:

  • Find its equation (y = mx + b) by reading off the slope and y-intercept.
  • Plug in a new x-value to predict y.
  • Read graphically: find x on the x-axis, go up to the line, then over to the y-axis.
